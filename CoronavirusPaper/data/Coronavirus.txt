                    &lt;h2 scrollto-destination=198623084 id=&quot;198623084&quot; class=&quot;abstract-title&quot; &gt;Abstract&lt;/h2&gt;
&lt;section class=&quot;abstract&quot;&gt;&lt;div class=&quot;sec&quot;&gt;&lt;div class=&quot;title&quot;&gt;Background&lt;/div&gt;&lt;p&gt;A novel coronavirus of zoonotic origin (2019-nCoV) has recently been identified in patients with acute respiratory disease. This virus is genetically similar to SARS coronavirus and bat SARS-like coronaviruses. The outbreak was initially detected in Wuhan, a major city of China, but has subsequently been detected in other provinces of China. Travel-associated cases have also been reported in a few other countries. Outbreaks in health care workers indicate human-to-human transmission. Molecular tests for rapid detection of this virus are urgently needed for early identification of infected patients.&lt;/p&gt;&lt;/div&gt;&lt;div class=&quot;js-collapsableEnd&quot;&gt;&lt;/div&gt;&lt;div class=&quot;sec&quot;&gt;&lt;div class=&quot;title&quot;&gt;Methods&lt;/div&gt;&lt;p&gt;We developed two 1-step quantitative real-time reverse-transcription PCR assays to detect two different regions (ORF1b and N) of the viral genome. The primer and probe sets were designed to react with this novel coronavirus and its closely related viruses, such as SARS coronavirus. These assays were evaluated using a panel of positive and negative controls. In addition, respiratory specimens from two 2019-nCoV-infected patients were tested.&lt;/p&gt;&lt;/div&gt;&lt;div class=&quot;js-collapsableEnd&quot;&gt;&lt;/div&gt;&lt;div class=&quot;sec&quot;&gt;&lt;div class=&quot;title&quot;&gt;Results&lt;/div&gt;&lt;p&gt;Using RNA extracted from cells infected by SARS coronavirus as a positive control, these assays were shown to have a dynamic range of at least seven orders of magnitude (2x10&lt;sup&gt;−4&lt;/sup&gt;-2000 TCID&lt;sub&gt;50&lt;/sub&gt;/reaction). Using DNA plasmids as positive standards, the detection limits of these assays were found to be below 10 copies per reaction. All negative control samples were negative in the assays. Samples from two 2019-nCoV-infected patients were positive in the tests.&lt;/p&gt;&lt;/div&gt;&lt;div class=&quot;js-collapsableEnd&quot;&gt;&lt;/div&gt;&lt;div class=&quot;sec&quot;&gt;&lt;div class=&quot;title&quot;&gt;Conclusions&lt;/div&gt;&lt;p&gt;The established assays can achieve a rapid detection of 2019n-CoV in human samples, thereby allowing early identification of patients.&lt;/p&gt;&lt;/div&gt;&lt;div class=&quot;js-collapsableEnd&quot;&gt;&lt;/div&gt;&lt;/section&gt;                        &lt;div class=&quot;article-metadata-panel clearfix&quot;&gt;&lt;/div&gt;
                    &lt;h2 scrollto-destination=198623085 id=&quot;198623085&quot; class=&quot;section-title&quot; &gt;Introduction&lt;/h2&gt;
&lt;p&gt;Coronaviruses can be classified into 4 genera (α, β, γ, and δ) and these viruses are detected in a wide range of animal species, including humans &lt;em&gt;(&lt;/em&gt;&lt;a reveal-id=&quot;hvaa029-B1&quot; data-open=&quot;hvaa029-B1&quot; class=&quot;link link-ref link-reveal xref-bibr&quot;&gt;1&lt;/a&gt;,&lt;em&gt;)&lt;/em&gt;. There are 6 previously known human coronaviruses that can be transmitted between humans. Human alphacoronaviruses, 229E and NL63, and betacoronaviruses, OC43 and HKU1, are common respiratory viruses usually causing mild upper respiratory diseases. In contrast to these, the other 2 human beta-coronaviruses, SARS and MERS coronaviruses, are highly pathogenic in humans. The case fatality rates of SARS and MERS are about 10% and 35%, respectively &lt;em&gt;(&lt;/em&gt;&lt;a reveal-id=&quot;hvaa029-B2&quot; data-open=&quot;hvaa029-B2&quot; class=&quot;link link-ref link-reveal xref-bibr&quot;&gt;2&lt;/a&gt;, &lt;a reveal-id=&quot;hvaa029-B3&quot; data-open=&quot;hvaa029-B3&quot; class=&quot;link link-ref link-reveal xref-bibr&quot;&gt;3&lt;/a&gt;,&lt;em&gt;)&lt;/em&gt;. SARS and MERS can be transmitted between humans and may cause major outbreaks in health care settings or in community settings. Both SARS and MERS coronaviruses are of zoonotic origins. MERS coronavirus is widely circulated in dromedary camels. Over 90% of dromedary camels in Middle East countries are seropositive for MERS coronavirus &lt;em&gt;(&lt;/em&gt;&lt;a reveal-id=&quot;hvaa029-B4&quot; data-open=&quot;hvaa029-B4&quot; class=&quot;link link-ref link-reveal xref-bibr&quot;&gt;4&lt;/a&gt;, &lt;a reveal-id=&quot;hvaa029-B5&quot; data-open=&quot;hvaa029-B5&quot; class=&quot;link link-ref link-reveal xref-bibr&quot;&gt;5&lt;/a&gt;,&lt;em&gt;)&lt;/em&gt;. Because of such a high prevalence in this animal species, sporadic zoonotic transmissions of MERS coronavirus from camels to humans have been repeatedly detected in the affected region since its discovery in 2012. For the SARS outbreak in 2003, exotic mammals in wet markets, such as palm civets and raccoon dogs, are believed to be the animal sources of the virus &lt;em&gt;(&lt;/em&gt;&lt;a reveal-id=&quot;hvaa029-B6&quot; data-open=&quot;hvaa029-B6&quot; class=&quot;link link-ref link-reveal xref-bibr&quot;&gt;6&lt;/a&gt;,&lt;em&gt;)&lt;/em&gt;. The detection of SARS coronavirus in these species led to a temporary ban of selling of these animals in 2004, thereby preventing additional spill-over events from animals to humans. But further surveillance studies indicated that these animals are rarely positive for SARS coronavirus in non-market settings, suggesting that they only served as intermediate hosts in the SARS event &lt;em&gt;(&lt;/em&gt;&lt;a reveal-id=&quot;hvaa029-B1&quot; data-open=&quot;hvaa029-B1&quot; class=&quot;link link-ref link-reveal xref-bibr&quot;&gt;1&lt;/a&gt;, &lt;a reveal-id=&quot;hvaa029-B7&quot; data-open=&quot;hvaa029-B7&quot; class=&quot;link link-ref link-reveal xref-bibr&quot;&gt;7&lt;/a&gt;,&lt;em&gt;)&lt;/em&gt;. Subsequent virus surveillances in wild animals identified a vast number of coronaviruses in bats &lt;em&gt;(&lt;/em&gt;&lt;a reveal-id=&quot;hvaa029-B8&quot; data-open=&quot;hvaa029-B8&quot; class=&quot;link link-ref link-reveal xref-bibr&quot;&gt;8&lt;/a&gt;,&lt;em&gt;)&lt;/em&gt;. Interestingly, several bat coronaviruses that are genetically similar to human SARS coronavirus were detected in horseshoe bats. This group of SARS-like bat coronaviruses, together with SARS coronavirus, form a unique clade under the subgenus &lt;em&gt;Sarbecovirus&lt;/em&gt;. It is now generally believed that SARS coronavirus is a recombinant virus between several bat SARS-like beta-coronaviruses &lt;em&gt;(&lt;/em&gt;&lt;a reveal-id=&quot;hvaa029-B9&quot; data-open=&quot;hvaa029-B9&quot; class=&quot;link link-ref link-reveal xref-bibr&quot;&gt;9&lt;/a&gt;,&lt;em&gt;)&lt;/em&gt;. Some of these bat coronaviruses are experimentally capable of infecting human cells in cultures &lt;em&gt;(&lt;/em&gt;&lt;a reveal-id=&quot;hvaa029-B9&quot; data-open=&quot;hvaa029-B9&quot; class=&quot;link link-ref link-reveal xref-bibr&quot;&gt;9&lt;/a&gt;&lt;em&gt;)&lt;/em&gt;, suggesting that these animal viruses and their recombinants can pose threats to human health.&lt;/p&gt;&lt;p&gt;In December 2019, a cluster of atypical pneumonia patients epidemiologically linked to a wholesale market in Wuhan was detected &lt;em&gt;(&lt;/em&gt;&lt;a reveal-id=&quot;hvaa029-B10&quot; data-open=&quot;hvaa029-B10&quot; class=&quot;link link-ref link-reveal xref-bibr&quot;&gt;10&lt;/a&gt;,&lt;em&gt;)&lt;/em&gt;. A novel beta-coronavirus named as 2019-novel coronavirus (2019-nCoV) has been identified in some of these patients. This virus is genetically similar to a bat virus in the subgenus &lt;em&gt;Sarbecovirus (&lt;/em&gt;&lt;a reveal-id=&quot;hvaa029-B11&quot; data-open=&quot;hvaa029-B11&quot; class=&quot;link link-ref link-reveal xref-bibr&quot;&gt;11&lt;/a&gt;,&lt;em&gt;)&lt;/em&gt;. As of 22 January 2020, over 200 cases, including 4 fatal ones, have been detected in Wuhan and other provinces &lt;em&gt;(&lt;/em&gt;&lt;a reveal-id=&quot;hvaa029-B12&quot; data-open=&quot;hvaa029-B12&quot; class=&quot;link link-ref link-reveal xref-bibr&quot;&gt;12&lt;/a&gt;,&lt;em&gt;)&lt;/em&gt;. In addition, confirmed patients with a recent travel history to Wuhan have been detected in multiple countries (Japan: 1, South Korea: 1, USA: 1, and Thailand: 2). Some of the confirmed cases have no epidemiological link to wet markets, suggesting the possibility of human-to-human transmissions and/or multiple spill-over events in different settings. At the time of preparing this article, a hospital outbreak had been reported &lt;em&gt;(&lt;/em&gt;&lt;a reveal-id=&quot;hvaa029-B13&quot; data-open=&quot;hvaa029-B13&quot; class=&quot;link link-ref link-reveal xref-bibr&quot;&gt;13&lt;/a&gt;&lt;em&gt;)&lt;/em&gt;. The spectrum of this disease in humans is yet to be fully determined. Signs of infection are highly nonspecific, and these include respiratory symptoms, fever, cough, dyspnea, and viral pneumonia. Thus, diagnostic tests specific for this infection are urgently needed for confirming suspected cases, screening patients and conducting virus surveillance.&lt;/p&gt;&lt;p&gt;In this study, we report the development of RT-PCR assays to detect this novel virus in human clinical specimens.&lt;/p&gt;                    &lt;h2 scrollto-destination=198623089 id=&quot;198623089&quot; class=&quot;section-title&quot; &gt;Materials and Methods&lt;/h2&gt;
                    &lt;h3 scrollto-destination=198623090 id=&quot;198623090&quot; class=&quot;section-title&quot; &gt;Primer and Probe Sequences&lt;/h3&gt;
&lt;p&gt;Two monoplex real-time RT-PCR assays targeting the ORF1b and N gene regions of 2019-nCoV were designed based on the first publicly available sequence in Genbank (Accession number: &lt;a class=&quot;link link-gen&quot; target=&quot;_blank&quot; href=&quot;https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/nuccore/MN908947&quot;&gt;MN908947&lt;/a&gt;). The downloaded sequence and those for SARS coronavirus, bat SARS-like coronaviruses, and other representative coronaviruses were edited and aligned. Phylogenetic analyses of these sequences were performed using the neighbor joining method in MEGA X &lt;em&gt;(&lt;/em&gt;&lt;a reveal-id=&quot;hvaa029-B14&quot; data-open=&quot;hvaa029-B14&quot; class=&quot;link link-ref link-reveal xref-bibr&quot;&gt;14&lt;/a&gt;&lt;em&gt;)&lt;/em&gt;. Two sequence regions (ORF1b and N) that are highly conserved amongst sarbecoviruses were selected for primer and probe designs. The primer and probe sequences for the ORF1b gene assay are: 5’-TGGGGYTTTACRGGTAACCT-3’ (Forward; Y = C/T, R = A/G), 5’-AACRCGCTTAACAAAGCACTC-3’ (Reverse; R = A/G) and 5′-TAGTTGTGATGCWATCATGACTAG-3′ (Probe in 5’-FAM/ZEN/3’-IBFQ format; W = A/T), whereas the primer and probe sequences for the N gene assay are: 5′-TAATCAGACAAGGAACTGATTA-3′ (Forward), 5′-CGAAGGTGTGACTTCCATG-3′ (Reverse) and 5′-GCAAATTGTGCAATTTGCGG-3′ (Probe in 5’-FAM/ZEN/3’-IBFQ format). The expected amplicon sizes of ORF1b and N gene assays are 132 bp and 110 bp, respectively. All primers and probes were purchased from a commercial source (Integrated DNA Technologies). The primer and probe sequences were subsequently confirmed to have perfect matches with other 2019-nCoV genome sequences available from Global Initiative on Sharing All Influenza Data (GISAID; &lt;a class=&quot;link link-uri openInAnotherWindow&quot; href=&quot;https://www.gisaid.org/&quot; target=&quot;_blank&quot;&gt;https://www.gisaid.org/&lt;/a&gt;; Accession numbers: EPI_ISL_402119, EPI_ISL_402120, EPI_ISL_402121, EPI_ISL_402123 and EPI_ISL_402124; Accessed 12 Jan 2020).&lt;/p&gt;                    &lt;h3 scrollto-destination=198623092 id=&quot;198623092&quot; class=&quot;section-title&quot; &gt;Quantitative RT-PCR Assay Condition&lt;/h3&gt;
&lt;p&gt;A typical 20 µL monoplex RT-PCR assay contained 5 μL of 4X master reaction mixture (TaqMan Fast Virus 1-Step Master Mix, ThermoFisher), 0.5 µmol/L of forward primer, 0.5 μmol/L of reverse primer, 0.25 μmo/L of probe, and 4 μl of RNA sample. RT-PCR reactions were conducted by a thermal cycler (ViiA7 Real-Time PCR system, ThermoFisher) with the following conditions: reverse transcription at 50°C for 5 min, inactivation of reverse transcriptase at 95°C for 20 s, 40 cycles of PCR amplification (Denaturing at 95°C for 5 s; Annealing/Extending at 60°C for 30 s). The time for each RT-PCR run was about 1 h and 15 min.&lt;/p&gt;&lt;p&gt;Viral RNA purification kit (QIAamp Viral RNA Mini Kit, Qiagen) and DNA plasmid purification kit (QIAprep Spin Miniprep Kit, Qiagen) were used for RNA and DNA extractions, respectively, as instructed by the manufacturer. For all RNA extractions, RNA was extracted from 140 µL of sample and eluted in 60 µL elution buffer containing poly(A) carrier RNA.&lt;/p&gt;                    &lt;h3 scrollto-destination=198623095 id=&quot;198623095&quot; class=&quot;section-title&quot; &gt;Tested Samples&lt;/h3&gt;
&lt;p&gt;Viral RNA extracted from SARS coronavirus-infected cells was used for positive controls. In addition, the RT-PCR products of SARS coronavirus generated by the ORF1b and N gene assays were cloned into plasmids. Serially diluted RNA or DNA samples were used to evaluate the performance of these assays. To determine the specificity of these assays, negative control samples were tested. These negative control samples were: 1) RNA extracted from cultured viruses: human coronaviruses (229E, OC43, and MERS), camel coronavirus (HKU23), human influenza A viruses (H1N1, H3N2, H5N1, and H7N9 subtypes), avian influenza (H1, H4, H6, and H9 subtypes), influenza B viruses (Yamagata and Victoria lineages), and adenovirus, 2) RNA extracted from retrospective human respiratory specimens previously tested positive for coronavirus (229E, HKU1, NL63, OC43), influenza A viruses (H1N1 and H3N2 subtypes), influenza B viruses (Yamagata and Victoria lineages), adenovirus, enterovirus, human parainfluenza virus (PIV1, 2, 3 and 4), respiratory syncytial virus, human metapneumovirus, rhinovirus and human bocavirus, and 3) RNA extracted from sputum samples from patients without respiratory viral infections (&lt;em&gt;n&lt;/em&gt; = 9). Culture supernatants of infected cells or clinical samples stored in standard virus transport media were subjected to a brief centrifugation (15,170 × g, 2 min) and 140 µL of supernatant from each sample was used for RNA extraction.&lt;/p&gt;&lt;p&gt;Two patients (Patients 1 and 2) suspected to be infected by 2019-nCoV in Beijing were included in this study. For Patient 1, a sputum sample was collected at day 5 post-onset of symptoms. The sputum sample stored in standard virus transport media was treated with an equal volume of Sputasol (ThermoFisher) before RNA extraction. For Patient 2, a throat swab sample was collected at day 3 post-onset of symptoms and stored in standard virus transport media. 140 µL of each aqueous sample was used for viral RNA extraction. Relevant clinical and epidemiological data for both patients were not accessible to this study. RNA extracted from these samples were serially diluted and tested by the assays.&lt;/p&gt;                    &lt;h2 scrollto-destination=198623098 id=&quot;198623098&quot; class=&quot;section-title&quot; &gt;Results&lt;/h2&gt;

Using additional sequence information from this clade of coronaviruses, we selected highly conserved ORF1b and N gene regions as our targets (&lt;a reveal-id=&quot;hvaa029-F1&quot; data-open=&quot;hvaa029-F1&quot; class=&quot;link link-reveal link-table xref-fig&quot;&gt;Fig. 1B&lt;/a&gt;). &lt;p&gt;Viral RNA from cells infected by SARS coronavirus or DNA plasmids containing the target sequences were positive in the assays as expected. Using viral RNA extracted from virus cultures with known titers, both assays have a dynamic range of at least seven orders of magnitude (2 x 10&lt;sup&gt;−4&lt;/sup&gt;-2000 TCID&lt;sub&gt;50&lt;/sub&gt;/reaction; data not shown). In our preliminary trial, reactions with ≥10 copies of this positive control plasmid were consistently positive in both of the assays (&lt;em&gt;n&lt;/em&gt; = 5). class=&quot;section-title&quot; &gt;Discussion&lt;/h2&gt;
&lt;p&gt;In this study, two assays targeting the viruses in the subgenus &lt;em&gt;Sarbecovirus&lt;/em&gt; were made and evaluated. We intentionally made these assays to be reactive to multiple viruses in this subgenus. This is because there is insufficient public information about the genetic diversity of 2019-nCoV in humans and animals. In 2003, SARS coronaviruses found in palm civets and humans were genetically highly similar, but distinct. For example, a partial deletion was detected in SARS coronavirus found in palm civets &lt;em&gt;(&lt;/em&gt;&lt;a reveal-id=&quot;hvaa029-B6&quot; data-open=&quot;hvaa029-B6&quot; class=&quot;link link-ref link-reveal xref-bibr&quot;&gt;6&lt;/a&gt;,&lt;em&gt;)&lt;/em&gt;. Similar subtle sequence variations were also observed in MERS coronavirus found in camels and humans &lt;em&gt;(&lt;/em&gt;&lt;a reveal-id=&quot;hvaa029-B15&quot; data-open=&quot;hvaa029-B15&quot; class=&quot;link link-ref link-reveal xref-bibr&quot;&gt;15&lt;/a&gt;&lt;em&gt;)&lt;/em&gt;. To avoid the possible scenario in which the genetic diversity of 2019-nCoV is much more diverse than appreciated, our primer and probe sets were designed to react with this clade of coronaviruses.&lt;/p&gt;&lt;p&gt;We evaluated our assays using a panel of positive and negative control samples. The assays were found to be sensitive and specific to only sarbecoviruses. We further used respiratory specimens from patients infected by 2019-nCoV to demonstrate the potential use of these tests. The tested clinical samples were different in nature (sputum vs. throat swab) and they were collected at different onset times (day 5 vs. day 3). Thus, it is premature to use these data to determine the viral replication kinetics in humans. Further systematic investigations on clinical specimens collected from 2019-nCoV-infected patients at different post-onset time points will be needed. Nonetheless, the results demonstrated the clinical value of these respiratory samples for molecular detection of 2019-nCoV. In addition, the N gene RT-PCR assay was found to be more sensitive in detecting 2019-nCoV RNA in the studied clinical samples. It is possible that these clinical samples might contain infected cells expressing subgenomic mRNA &lt;em&gt;(&lt;/em&gt;&lt;a reveal-id=&quot;hvaa029-B16&quot; data-open=&quot;hvaa029-B16&quot; class=&quot;link link-ref link-reveal xref-bibr&quot;&gt;16&lt;/a&gt;&lt;em&gt;)&lt;/em&gt;, resulting in more N gene copies in the samples.&lt;/p&gt;&lt;p&gt;With the exception of 2019-nCoV and SARS coronavirus, none of the sarbecoviruses have been previously detected in humans. SARS was eliminated in humans and the last reported human SARS case was detected in 2004 &lt;em&gt;(&lt;/em&gt;&lt;a reveal-id=&quot;hvaa029-B17&quot; data-open=&quot;hvaa029-B17&quot; class=&quot;link link-ref link-reveal xref-bibr&quot;&gt;17&lt;/a&gt;,&lt;em&gt;)&lt;/em&gt;. Individuals with samples that are positive in these RT-PCR assays should be considered to be infected by the 2019-nCoV or its related animal coronaviruses. Based on their detection performances, the N gene RT-PCR is recommended as a screening assay, and the Orf1b assay is recommended as a confirmatory one. Using a diagnostic algorithm similar to MERS, an N gene positive/Orf1b negative result should be regarded as indeterminate and the case is recommended to be referred to a WHO reference lab for further testing &lt;em&gt;(&lt;/em&gt;&lt;a reveal-id=&quot;hvaa029-B18&quot; data-open=&quot;hvaa029-B18&quot; class=&quot;link link-ref link-reveal xref-bibr&quot;&gt;18&lt;/a&gt;&lt;em&gt;)&lt;/em&gt;. In the event of having positive PCR results, sequence analyses of positive amplicons can help to confirm the result and to distinguish between 2019-nCoV and other genetically related coronaviruses (e.g., SARS coronavirus).&lt;/p&gt;&lt;p&gt;The biosafety classification of 2019-nCoV is yet to be fully defined. Clinical samples for molecular tests are recommended to be handled using biosafety level 2 practices &lt;em&gt;(&lt;/em&gt;&lt;a reveal-id=&quot;hvaa029-B19&quot; data-open=&quot;hvaa029-B19&quot; class=&quot;link link-ref link-reveal xref-bibr&quot;&gt;19&lt;/a&gt;&lt;em&gt;)&lt;/em&gt;. As SARS coronavirus is classified as a biosafety level 3 (BSL3) pathogen, we therefore intentionally used DNA plasmids as positive controls in these tests. This can avoid distributing SARS coronavirus genomic RNA which has the potential of generating infectious clones via reverse genetics. In addition, this can also help to distribute positive controls to laboratories at different geographical regions in a more cost-effective and robust manner. These DNA plasmid controls are available upon request.&lt;/p&gt;&lt;p&gt;During the reviewing process of this manuscript, Corman and colleagues reported the use synthetic constructs and SARS virus to develop RT-PCR assays for 2019-nCoV detection &lt;em&gt;(&lt;/em&gt;&lt;a reveal-id=&quot;hvaa029-B20&quot; data-open=&quot;hvaa029-B20&quot; class=&quot;link link-ref link-reveal xref-bibr&quot;&gt;20&lt;/a&gt;&lt;em&gt;)&lt;/em&gt;.&lt;/p&gt;                    &lt;h2 scrollto-destination=198623115 id=&quot;198623115&quot; class=&quot;backsection-title&quot; &gt;Disclosures&lt;/h2&gt;
